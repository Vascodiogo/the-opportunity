How it works on-chain

AuthOnce has two smart contracts on Base Network:

ContractRole
MerchantRegistryStores approved merchants and their treasury wallet addresses.
SubscriptionVaultHolds subscriber authorisations (EIP-2612 permits) and executes payment pulls on each billing date.

When a subscriber authorises a subscription:

  1. They sign an EIP-2612 permit off-chain — a cryptographic signature that authorises the vault to pull a specific amount of USDC on a recurring schedule.
  2. The subscriber's vault is funded with exactly one billing cycle's worth of USDC. No more.
  3. On each billing date, the keeper bot calls executePull() on the SubscriptionVault, which atomically transfers USDC from the subscriber vault to the merchant treasury (99.5%) and the AuthOnce protocol treasury (0.5%).

Atomic and non-custodial. The keeper never holds funds. AuthOnce never holds funds. The subscriber's USDC goes directly to your treasury in a single transaction that either fully succeeds or fully reverts.

Treasury wallet setup

Your merchant address is where USDC lands on every successful payment. You can use:

  • An EOA (regular wallet) — MetaMask, Coinbase Wallet, etc.
  • A Safe multisig — recommended for DAOs (see below)
  • Any EVM-compatible smart contract wallet that can receive ERC-20 tokens

Set it once. Your treasury wallet is your registered merchant address on AuthOnce. All USDC from all subscriptions goes here. You can update it later via a two-step admin transfer, but it requires a proposal and acceptance from the new address — plan accordingly.

Safe multisig (recommended for DAOs)

For DAOs and multi-stakeholder teams, we strongly recommend registering a Safe multisig as your merchant address. This means:

  • No single key controls your subscription revenue
  • Treasury transfers require M-of-N signatures from your team
  • Compatible with existing DAO tooling (Snapshot, Tally, Aragon)
  • AuthOnce's own protocol treasury uses a 2/2 Safe on Base Mainnet

Setup steps

1

Create a Safe on Base Network

Go to app.safe.global, select Base Network, add signers, set threshold. Fund with a small amount of ETH for gas.

2

Register the Safe address as your merchant

Apply to AuthOnce with your Safe address as the merchant wallet. This is the address that will receive USDC on every payment.

3

Keep a small ETH balance in the Safe

USDC arrives directly via the keeper — no gas required on your side. But if you want to move funds out of the Safe, signers will need ETH for gas.

DAO memberships

DAOs collecting recurring membership fees from contributors or community members are an ideal AuthOnce use case. A typical setup:

  • Create a "Member" product at your monthly or annual membership rate
  • Share the pay link in your Discord, Notion, or governance forum
  • Members connect their wallet and sign once — no further action needed
  • USDC arrives in the DAO treasury wallet on each billing date
  • Failed payments enter a grace period and retry automatically — no manual intervention

You receive a webhook event on every payment, cancellation, and expiry — wire these to your member management system to automatically grant or revoke access.

Tool & API subscriptions

If you sell tooling, data access, or API access to other Web3 teams or DAOs, AuthOnce is a natural fit. Your customers are already on-chain — they have wallets, they hold USDC. There is no fiat onramp friction.

Compared to collecting payment through invoice or token transfer:

  • Automatic — no manual renewals, no "please top up your subscription" messages
  • Verifiable — you can prove payment on Basescan without sharing internal data
  • Predictable — grace period dunning recovers failed payments before cancellation

Contributor retainers

AuthOnce can also be used in reverse — a DAO paying contributors a recurring amount is a standard outgoing payment pattern, not an incoming subscription. For contributor payments going out of your treasury, you should use a tool like Superfluid, Sablier, or Parcel.

AuthOnce handles incoming recurring revenue — subscribers (or contributing DAOs) paying your DAO. If a contributor wants to pay for access to your DAO's resources or data, AuthOnce is the right tool.

Verifying payments on-chain

Every payment executed by AuthOnce has a transaction hash. You can verify any payment on Basescan:

  1. Go to basescan.org
  2. Search for your treasury wallet address
  3. Under ERC-20 Token Txns, filter by USDC — every AuthOnce payment appears here
  4. Each transaction shows: from (subscriber vault), to (your treasury), amount, timestamp, block number

This means your DAO's revenue is independently auditable by any token holder or governance participant without needing access to your internal dashboard.

No chargebacks by design

On-chain USDC transfers are cryptographically final. Once executePull() succeeds on-chain, the payment cannot be reversed, disputed, or charged back. There is no payment processor standing between the subscriber and your treasury that could freeze, reverse, or confiscate funds.

This is structurally different from custodial billing processors where chargebacks, disputes, and account freezes are a constant operational risk — especially for Web3 businesses which some processors consider high-risk.

Non-custodial architecture

AuthOnce never holds merchant or subscriber funds. The protocol operates as follows:

  • Subscriber USDC stays in the subscriber's own vault address at all times until a payment executes
  • On execution, USDC moves directly to the merchant treasury — AuthOnce is never an intermediary holder
  • The AuthOnce keeper bot is a transaction executor only — it cannot spend subscriber funds on anything other than the agreed subscription amount

MiCA positioning

AuthOnce is designed as a software protocol, not a payment service provider. Because the protocol never holds funds and operates entirely through on-chain smart contract execution, AuthOnce does not classify as a Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) under MiCA. This is a deliberate architectural choice.

The protocol contracts are licensed under BUSL-1.1 and publicly auditable. For merchants operating in the EU, AuthOnce's non-custodial design means you are interacting directly with the Base Network blockchain — not with a regulated intermediary holding your assets.
