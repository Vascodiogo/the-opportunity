Non-custodial vs custodial crypto subscriptions — why it matters.

For subscription-based Web3 platforms, the choice of billing infrastructure is a security decision, not just a product decision. Here's what that means in practice.

Every recurring crypto payment solution fits into one of two categories: custodial or non-custodial. The difference isn't cosmetic. It determines who controls your revenue, who can freeze your payouts, and what happens if the platform you depend on shuts down.

This page explains the architectural difference, the real-world implications, and why an increasing number of Web3 merchants are switching to non-custodial subscription protocols.

The architecture difference

Custodial Subscriber wallet → Third-party pool → (delayed) → Merchant wallet
AuthOnce Subscriber wallet → Smart contract (atomic) → Merchant wallet

In the custodial model, subscriber funds pass through a third-party wallet before reaching you. That intermediary holds your revenue — sometimes for hours, sometimes for days. They decide when to release it, under what conditions, and whether to release it at all.

In the non-custodial model, there is no intermediate state. The smart contract executes a single atomic transaction: funds leave the subscriber's wallet and arrive in the merchant's wallet simultaneously. No pool. No holding period. No intermediary with discretion over your revenue.

"Non-custodial isn't a feature we added. It's a constraint we designed in — enforced by the contract, not by policy."

The custody trap in practice

The risk of custodial platforms isn't theoretical. In 2022–2023, multiple centralized crypto platforms froze customer withdrawals during market stress. In each case, the platform's terms of service gave them legal authority to hold funds — authority that customers discovered existed only after they needed to withdraw.

A subscription gateway that holds your revenue has the same structural authority. If their bank relationship is terminated, if they face regulatory action, or if they simply decide to change their payout schedule — your recurring revenue is affected.

Non-custodial architecture removes this risk entirely. AuthOnce has no authority over merchant funds because it never touches them. The protocol collects its 0.5% fee atomically in the same transaction — it cannot collect more, and it cannot hold what it collects.

Why gasless matters for subscriber retention

Earlier Web3 billing models required subscribers to pay gas on every renewal. On Ethereum mainnet, this could mean $5–50 per monthly payment. On Base, gas is sub-cent — but the friction of an active transaction still exists.

AuthOnce uses EIP-2612 permit signatures. Subscribers sign a single off-chain message at signup. No gas. No transaction. From that point, every billing cycle executes automatically — the subscriber never needs to interact with their wallet again unless they want to cancel.

What about Base Spend Permissions?

Base's native Spend Permissions are a non-custodial recurring payment primitive — and a good one. But they have a critical limitation: they require a Coinbase Smart Wallet. Subscribers using MetaMask, Ledger, hardware wallets, or any non-Coinbase wallet cannot use Spend Permissions.

AuthOnce uses EIP-2612 — a standard supported by every major EVM wallet. Your subscriber base doesn't need to switch wallets to use your subscription product.

Additionally, AuthOnce adds the dunning and grace period layer that Spend Permissions don't provide natively — the difference between losing a subscriber on the first failed payment and recovering them automatically.
