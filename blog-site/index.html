Six months ago I didn't know Solidity. I had a day job in Switzerland, evenings and weekends, and a problem I couldn't stop thinking about: why is it still impossible to accept a recurring crypto payment without building custom infrastructure from scratch?

Stripe solved this for fiat in 2011. It's 2026. The stablecoin economy is real — Circle reported $250B in USDC circulation. Meta is routing $3B a year in creator payouts through USDC across 160+ countries. And yet if you're a SaaS founder who wants to accept monthly USDC subscriptions from your users, you have two options: build it yourself, or don't.

I chose a third option. I built AuthOnce. Here's what I learned along the way — and why the architecture of recurring payments matters more than most people realise.

Why on-chain subscription billing has been broken

Every crypto payment you've ever made is a push payment. You initiate it. You sign a transaction. You send. That works perfectly for one-time payments.

Subscriptions are pull payments. The merchant initiates the charge on a schedule. The customer doesn't need to do anything after the initial authorisation. This is how Stripe, your bank, your electricity company — every recurring billing system in the world — works.

Blockchain, by design, doesn't have a native pull mechanism. Wallets can't be charged without the owner's active participation. This creates an enormous gap: the entire recurring revenue model of the modern software economy simply doesn't exist on-chain.

"The entire recurring revenue model of the modern software economy simply doesn't exist on-chain."

Every protocol that's tried to solve this has made the same mistake: they ask subscribers to lock funds into a custodial vault upfront. The protocol holds your money and releases it to merchants on a schedule. It works — until it doesn't. Until a contract gets exploited. Until a regulatory change makes custody legally untenable. Until the terms change and merchants find themselves subject to fee increases they never agreed to.

The custody trap

The custodial model isn't just technically fragile — it creates serious legal exposure. In most jurisdictions, holding customer funds on behalf of a merchant makes you a payment institution. You need licences. Compliance infrastructure. A legal team.

More importantly for subscribers: your funds are not yours the moment they enter a custodial vault. You're trusting the protocol operator, the smart contract, and the team behind it — simultaneously.

AuthOnce never holds funds. Not for a millisecond. Every payment is a single atomic transaction: subscriber wallet → merchant wallet. The protocol collects its fee in the same transaction. There is no intermediate state where anyone other than the subscriber or merchant holds value.

This isn't a product feature we added. It's a constraint we designed in from the start — and one we cannot remove even if we wanted to, because it's enforced by the contract itself.

Gasless crypto subscriptions: authorise once, pay forever

The name isn't just a tagline. It describes the exact user experience we designed around.

A subscriber connects their wallet and signs a single authorisation. No gas fee. No on-chain transaction at any point during setup. The signature is stored by our keeper infrastructure. On each billing date, the keeper executes the payment — pulling exactly the subscription amount, atomically, directly to the merchant's wallet.

From the subscriber's perspective: set it and forget it. From the merchant's perspective: predictable, automated revenue in stablecoins, settled instantly, with no chargebacks possible.

If the subscriber revokes their authorisation, payments stop immediately. If their balance is insufficient, the payment fails gracefully — and the grace period begins.

The grace period problem nobody talks about

Failed payments are the silent killer of subscription businesses. Stripe's data shows that involuntary churn — customers who leave because their card failed, not because they wanted to cancel — accounts for up to 40% of total churn for some SaaS businesses.

On-chain, this problem is potentially worse. A wallet might be temporarily underfunded. Network conditions might cause a transaction to fail. Without a recovery mechanism, a single failed payment means a permanently lost subscriber.

AuthOnce has a programmable grace period built directly into the protocol. When a payment fails, the subscription enters a grace state. Our keeper retries daily for the configured window — up to 30 days. During this period, access isn't immediately revoked and the subscriber gets a window to resolve the issue. Most do.

This behaviour is enforced on-chain, not by a backend system that can fail, be changed, or be selectively applied. Every merchant gets the same dunning logic. It cannot be disabled.

USDC subscription payments on Base: why the fee is hardcoded

This was a deliberate architectural decision, not a pricing choice.

Traditional payment processors can change their fees. They have. They will again. A merchant building their pricing model on top of Stripe's 2.9% fee has no guarantee that fee won't change next quarter.

The creator economy angle

While building this, Meta announced it would route $3B per year in creator payouts through USDC across 160+ countries. Millions of creators are receiving stablecoins as primary income.

Those creators need somewhere to spend it — on the SaaS tools, AI subscriptions, and professional services they use every day. Not through a CEX. Not by selling to fiat first. Directly, from their wallet, to a merchant who accepts USDC.

AuthOnce is the spending layer for that economy. A merchant who integrates AuthOnce gets access to a growing base of stablecoin holders who can subscribe with a single authorisation, no bank account required, no FX conversion, no intermediary taking a cut beyond our 0.5%.

Meta solved the disbursement problem. AuthOnce solves the spending problem.

When a creator in Brazil, Nigeria, or Indonesia receives USDC from Meta, they can subscribe to any AuthOnce-integrated merchant — anywhere in the world — without touching a bank or a centralised exchange.

That's what financial inclusion actually looks like in practice.
