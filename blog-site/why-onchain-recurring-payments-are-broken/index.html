Non-custodial crypto subscriptions on Base — how we built what didn't exist.

Every Web3 subscription protocol has the same fatal flaw. Here's what we built instead — and why the architecture of recurring payments matters more than most people realise.

VD
Vasco Diogo · 8 min read

When I started building AuthOnce, I assumed the hard part would be the smart contracts. It wasn't. The hard part was realising that no one had solved the fundamental problem correctly — and that the reason was architectural, not technical.

Recurring crypto payments have existed in various forms since 2017. None of them works the way a SaaS subscription should. Here's why — and what we built instead.

The problem every Web3 subscription gets wrong

There are two dominant approaches to on-chain recurring payments. Both are broken.

Model 1: Unlimited approval. The subscriber approves the merchant's contract to pull unlimited tokens. Simple — but it means the subscriber has handed over permanent, uncapped access to their wallet. One contract exploit, one rogue merchant, and the subscriber's entire balance is gone. Security-conscious users reject this immediately.

Model 2: Manual renewal. The subscriber pays for each period manually. This eliminates the approval risk but destroys the subscription UX entirely. A subscription where the customer has to manually pay each month is not a subscription — it's an invoice.

"The best subscription is one the subscriber forgets about. That's impossible if they have to sign a transaction every month."

Both models sacrifice something essential. Unlimited approvals sacrifice security. Manual renewals sacrifice automation. Neither produces something a real SaaS business can build on.

What EIP-2612 actually changes

EIP-2612 is a permit standard for ERC-20 tokens. It allows a user to sign a typed off-chain message authorising a specific contract to pull a specific amount of tokens by a specific deadline — without an on-chain approval transaction.

The signature is gasless for the subscriber. The permit can be scoped: exact amount, exact spender, expiry date. It is not an unlimited approval. It is cryptographically bound to the parameters the subscriber agreed to at signup.

USDC on Base implements EIP-2612. So does EURC, and most modern stablecoins. This is the primitive AuthOnce is built on.

Why non-custodial is non-negotiable

Every custodial crypto payment gateway introduces a party between the subscriber's money and the merchant's wallet. That party can freeze funds, delay payouts, change terms, or face regulatory action. History has demonstrated all of these outcomes repeatedly.

AuthOnce never holds funds. The protocol fee — 0.5%, hardcoded in the contract — is collected atomically in the same transaction as the merchant payment. There is no pool, no escrow, no settlement delay. If AuthOnce ceased to exist tomorrow, every active subscription would continue executing correctly because the logic lives on-chain.

This is not a marketing position. It has real regulatory implications. Because AuthOnce never takes custody of subscriber or merchant funds, it operates as a software protocol — not a payment institution. This distinction matters significantly under MiCA and under Portuguese financial regulation, where we are incorporating.

The dunning layer nobody built

Failed payments are the primary source of involuntary churn in any subscription business. In fiat SaaS, Stripe Billing handles this with smart retries, email sequences, and grace periods. In crypto, almost nothing exists.

We built the dunning layer directly into the smart contract. Each subscription has a configurable grace period — 1 to 30 days, set at creation. If a payment fails, the subscription enters a grace state. The keeper bot retries daily. If the subscriber tops up their wallet during the grace window, the next retry succeeds and the subscription continues as normal.

What we support — and what we don't

AuthOnce supports USDC, USDT, DAI, and EURC. These are the stablecoins that implement EIP-2612 and have sufficient liquidity on Base. We deliberately do not support WETH or cbBTC for subscriptions — volatile assets create pricing problems that stablecoins don't have.

Merchants set prices in stablecoin or fiat equivalent. If a merchant prices in EUR, AuthOnce fetches the live USDC/EUR rate at checkout and locks in the USDC amount at that moment. The subscriber always pays exactly what was shown — no slippage, no surprises.

Fiat subscribers, crypto merchants

Not every subscriber will have USDC. We built a Stripe Checkout integration that lets subscribers pay by card, MB Way, Multibanco, or SEPA bank transfer. The fiat payment funds a non-custodial vault with the exact USDC amount — and from that point, the on-chain subscription machinery takes over identically.

For the merchant, every subscriber looks the same: a vault address holding USDC, a billing interval, and a grace period configuration. Whether the subscriber paid with a credit card in Lisbon or a Coinbase Wallet in San Francisco is irrelevant to the protocol.
